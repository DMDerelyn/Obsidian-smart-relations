# Ethics in Artificial Intelligence

As AI systems increasingly make or influence decisions that affect human lives, establishing robust ethical frameworks has become urgent. This note explores the key principles, philosophical foundations, and practical challenges of AI ethics.

## Core Ethical Principles

### Fairness
AI systems should treat individuals and groups **equitably**, avoiding unjust discrimination. However, fairness itself is a contested concept with multiple, sometimes contradictory definitions:

- **Demographic parity**: Equal prediction rates across groups
- **Equal opportunity**: Equal true positive rates across groups
- **Individual fairness**: Similar individuals should receive similar predictions
- **Calibration**: Predicted probabilities should match observed frequencies for all groups

Research has shown that it is mathematically impossible to satisfy all fairness criteria simultaneously (the "impossibility theorem"), forcing designers to make value-laden choices about which form of fairness to prioritize.

### Transparency

AI systems should be **understandable** to those affected by them. Transparency operates at multiple levels:

- **Algorithmic transparency**: Can the system's decision-making process be explained?
- **Data transparency**: What data was used for training, and how was it collected?
- **Outcome transparency**: Are individuals informed when AI is used in decisions about them?
- **Corporate transparency**: Are organizations open about their AI practices and limitations?

The tension between transparency and proprietary interests is significant. Companies may resist disclosing models that represent significant competitive advantages.

### Accountability

When AI systems cause harm, there must be clear **mechanisms for responsibility**:

- Who is accountable: the developer, deployer, user, or the organization?
- What recourse do affected individuals have?
- How are errors detected, reported, and corrected?
- What governance structures oversee AI deployment?

Accountability requires both technical mechanisms (audit logs, monitoring systems) and institutional structures (oversight boards, regulatory bodies, #ethical-review processes).

## Philosophical Foundations

AI ethics draws on several philosophical traditions:

### Consequentialism
Evaluates AI by its **outcomes**. An AI system is ethical if it produces the greatest good for the greatest number. Challenges: How do we measure and compare harms and benefits? Whose outcomes count?

### Deontological Ethics
Focuses on **rules and duties** regardless of outcomes. An AI system must respect fundamental rights (privacy, autonomy, non-discrimination) even if violating them would produce better aggregate outcomes.

### Virtue Ethics
Asks what kind of **character and values** AI development should embody. Encourages developers to cultivate virtues like honesty, humility, and care in their professional practice.

### Care Ethics
Emphasizes **relationships and context**. AI systems should be evaluated by how they affect the most vulnerable and whether they strengthen or weaken human relationships and community bonds.

## Practical Challenges

### Value Alignment
How do we ensure AI systems pursue goals that align with human values? The specification problem is profound: human values are complex, context-dependent, and sometimes contradictory.

### Dual Use
AI technologies developed for beneficial purposes can be repurposed for harmful ones. Facial recognition developed for unlocking phones can be used for mass surveillance.

### Global Perspectives
Ethical frameworks developed in Western contexts may not reflect values and priorities of other cultures. Meaningful AI ethics must incorporate diverse global perspectives.

### The Pace Problem
AI technology advances faster than ethical frameworks and regulations can develop. By the time ethical guidelines are established, the technology may have already evolved significantly.
