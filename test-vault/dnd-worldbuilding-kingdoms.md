# Worldbuilding: Kingdoms and Politics

A compelling D&D campaign setting requires well-developed political structures, factions with competing interests, and trade networks that give the world economic life. This note outlines frameworks and examples for kingdom-level worldbuilding.

## Major Kingdoms Framework

When designing kingdoms, consider these dimensions for each:

### The Valorian Empire (Example)

- **Government**: Hereditary monarchy with a council of noble houses
- **Economy**: Agricultural heartland with iron mining in the northern provinces
- **Military**: Professional standing army of 40,000, supplemented by noble levies
- **Religion**: State worship of the Sun God; other faiths tolerated but not promoted
- **Culture**: Emphasizes duty, honor, and martial prowess; literacy common among nobility

### The Free Cities of the Coast

- **Government**: Merchant republics governed by elected trade councils
- **Economy**: Maritime trade, banking, and shipbuilding
- **Military**: Mercenary companies and powerful navies; no standing armies
- **Religion**: Pluralistic; temples to all gods welcome as they attract diverse traders
- **Culture**: Cosmopolitan, wealth-oriented; social mobility through commerce

## Political Factions

Factions create conflict and adventure hooks. Effective factions have:

1. **Clear goals** that sometimes align and sometimes conflict with other factions
2. **Resources** they can bring to bear (military, economic, magical, informational)
3. **Internal divisions** that players can exploit or navigate
4. **A public face** and a **hidden agenda**

Example factions:
- *The Iron Covenant*: Militant anti-magic organization seeking to regulate spellcasters
- *The Emerald Court*: Druidic council protecting ancient forests from imperial expansion
- *The Shadow Market*: Underground network of smugglers, spies, and information brokers

## Trade Routes

Trade routes are the arteries of a living world. They determine:

- Which cities are wealthy and which are struggling
- Where bandits and monsters congregate (along profitable routes)
- How information and culture spread between regions
- Strategic chokepoints that drive #political-conflict

Key trade goods to consider: grain, metals, lumber, textiles, spices, magical components, and exotic creatures. A disruption to any major trade route can serve as a campaign-level adventure hook.

## Diplomacy and War

Kingdoms rarely exist in isolation. Map out relationships between nations:

- **Alliances** (military, economic, dynastic marriage)
- **Rivalries** (border disputes, resource competition, historical grievances)
- **Dependencies** (vassal states, trade dependencies, magical pacts)

These relationships should shift over the course of a campaign, reacting to player actions and world events.
