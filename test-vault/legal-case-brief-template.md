# Legal Case Brief Template

A case brief is a structured summary of a court decision. Briefing cases is a fundamental skill for law students and legal professionals, enabling quick reference and deeper understanding of legal principles.

## Template Structure

### 1. Case Citation

**Format**: *Party v. Party*, Volume Reporter Page (Court Year)

**Example**: *Marbury v. Madison*, 5 U.S. (1 Cranch) 137 (1803)

Always include the full citation so the case can be located. Use proper Bluebook format for formal briefs.

### 2. Facts

Summarize the **relevant facts** that gave rise to the legal dispute. Focus on:

- Who are the parties and what is their relationship?
- What happened chronologically?
- What is the procedural history? (How did this case reach this court?)
- Which facts are **legally significant** --- i.e., facts that the court relied on in its reasoning?

**Tip**: Distinguish between background facts (context) and operative facts (facts that trigger legal rules). Include only facts that matter to the court's analysis.

### 3. Issue

State the **legal question** the court must answer. Frame it as a yes/no question that incorporates the relevant rule and key facts:

**Poor**: "Was the defendant liable?"  
**Better**: "Whether a manufacturer owes a duty of care to the ultimate consumer of its product when the product is sold through an intermediary."

A case may present **multiple issues**. List each separately and address them in the order the court does.

### 4. Holding

The court's **answer to the issue** --- the legal rule established by the case. State it clearly and specifically:

**Example**: "Yes. A manufacturer owes a duty of care to any person who might foreseeably be harmed by its product, regardless of privity of contract."

### 5. Reasoning

This is the most important section. Explain **why** the court reached its holding:

- What legal rules, precedents, or statutes did the court apply?
- How did the court interpret ambiguous language or resolve conflicts between authorities?
- What policy considerations influenced the decision?
- How did the court distinguish or follow prior cases?

### 6. Concurrences and Dissents

Note significant concurring or dissenting opinions, especially if they:

- Propose an alternative legal framework
- Highlight weaknesses in the majority's reasoning
- Foreshadow future legal developments

### 7. Personal Notes

Add your own analysis:

- How does this case relate to the course theme or other cases?
- Do you agree with the court's reasoning?
- What are the implications for future cases?

## Example Brief

```
CASE: Palsgraf v. Long Island Railroad Co., 248 N.Y. 339 (1928)

FACTS: Railroad guards helped a passenger board a moving
train, dislodging a package of fireworks. The explosion
caused scales to fall on plaintiff Palsgraf, injuring her.
Trial court and appellate division found for plaintiff.

ISSUE: Whether a defendant owes a duty of care to a
plaintiff who is injured by unforeseeable consequences
of the defendant's negligence toward another person.

HOLDING: No. Liability requires a breach of duty owed
to the specific plaintiff; negligence in the air is not
actionable.

REASONING: Cardozo J. held that duty is relational ---
the defendant's conduct must be negligent with respect
to the injured party specifically. The risk of harm to
Palsgraf was not foreseeable from the guards' actions
with the boarding passenger.
```

## Tips for Effective Briefing

- Keep briefs to **one page** for classroom use
- Use your own words; do not copy the court's language verbatim
- Focus on the #legal-reasoning rather than the facts
- Update briefs after class discussion to incorporate insights
- Build a personal case database organized by legal topic for exam preparation
