# Contract Law Basics

A contract is a legally enforceable agreement between two or more parties. Contract law provides the framework for private agreements that underpin virtually all commercial activity. Understanding its fundamentals is essential for anyone engaged in business, law, or negotiations.

## Elements of a Valid Contract

For a contract to be legally enforceable, it must contain all of the following elements:

### 1. Offer
A clear, definite proposal made by one party (the **offeror**) to another (the **offeree**), indicating willingness to enter into a binding agreement on specified terms. An offer must be:

- **Definite**: Terms must be clear enough for a court to enforce
- **Communicated**: The offeree must be aware of the offer
- **Distinguished from invitations to treat**: Advertisements, price lists, and displays are generally invitations to negotiate, not binding offers

An offer can be terminated by rejection, counteroffer, lapse of time, revocation (before acceptance), or death of either party.

### 2. Acceptance
Unconditional agreement to all terms of the offer. Key rules:

- Must **mirror** the offer exactly (the "mirror image rule")
- A response that changes terms is a **counteroffer**, not an acceptance
- Silence generally does not constitute acceptance
- The **mailbox rule**: Acceptance is effective when dispatched (for mail), not when received

### 3. Consideration
Something of value exchanged between the parties. Each party must give something and receive something:

- Can be money, goods, services, a promise, or forbearance (agreeing not to do something)
- Must be **bargained for**, not a gift or past action
- Courts generally do not evaluate the **adequacy** of consideration --- a peppercorn is sufficient

### 4. Capacity
Both parties must have the legal ability to contract:

- **Minors** (under 18) can generally void contracts (voidable, not void)
- **Mentally incapacitated** persons may lack capacity
- **Intoxicated** persons may lack capacity if the other party knew

### 5. Legality
The contract's purpose must be legal. Contracts for illegal activities (drug deals, price-fixing) are void and unenforceable.

## Breach of Contract

A breach occurs when a party fails to perform their obligations. Types include:

- **Material breach**: A significant failure that goes to the heart of the contract, excusing the other party from performance
- **Minor breach**: A less significant failure; the non-breaching party must still perform but can sue for damages
- **Anticipatory breach**: One party indicates in advance that they will not perform

## Remedies

When a breach occurs, courts may award:

1. **Compensatory damages**: Money to put the injured party in the position they would have been in had the contract been performed
2. **Consequential damages**: Foreseeable losses resulting from the breach
3. **Specific performance**: A court order requiring the breaching party to perform (rare; typically for unique goods or real estate)
4. **Rescission**: Canceling the contract and restoring both parties to their pre-contract positions
5. **Liquidated damages**: Pre-agreed damage amounts specified in the contract itself

The fundamental goal of #contract-remedies is to make the injured party whole, not to punish the breaching party.
