# Intellectual Property Law

Intellectual property (IP) law protects creations of the mind, granting creators exclusive rights over their work for a limited period. IP law balances incentivizing innovation and creativity with ensuring public access to knowledge and culture.

## Copyright

Copyright protects **original works of authorship** fixed in a tangible medium of expression:

### What is Protected
- Literary works (books, articles, software code)
- Musical and dramatic works
- Visual art (paintings, photographs, sculptures)
- Architectural works
- Sound recordings and film

### What is Not Protected
- Ideas, facts, and concepts (only the *expression* is protected)
- Works of the US government
- Common words, phrases, and symbols
- Functional aspects of useful articles

### Duration
- Works created after 1978: Life of the author + 70 years
- Works for hire: 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation
- After copyright expires, works enter the **public domain**

### Rights Granted
The copyright holder has exclusive rights to reproduce, distribute, perform, display, and create derivative works. These rights can be licensed, sold, or transferred.

## Trademarks

Trademarks protect **words, symbols, designs, or combinations** that identify the source of goods or services:

- Must be **distinctive** (arbitrary or fanciful marks are strongest)
- Registration with the USPTO provides nationwide protection and legal presumptions
- Trademarks can last **indefinitely** as long as they are used in commerce and properly maintained
- Infringement occurs when another's use causes a **likelihood of confusion** among consumers

## Patents

Patents protect **inventions and discoveries** that are novel, non-obvious, and useful:

- **Utility patents**: Protect functional inventions (machines, processes, compositions of matter). Last 20 years from filing.
- **Design patents**: Protect ornamental designs of functional items. Last 15 years from grant.
- **Plant patents**: Protect new plant varieties. Last 20 years from filing.

The patent application must include a **detailed disclosure** teaching others how to make and use the invention. This public disclosure is the tradeoff for the temporary monopoly.

## Trade Secrets

Trade secrets protect **confidential business information** that derives value from its secrecy:

- No registration required; protection lasts as long as secrecy is maintained
- Examples: formulas (Coca-Cola), algorithms, customer lists, manufacturing processes
- Protected by the Defend Trade Secrets Act (federal) and state laws based on the Uniform Trade Secrets Act
- Misappropriation through theft, breach of contract, or espionage is actionable

## Fair Use

The #fair-use doctrine provides a critical limitation on copyright, allowing limited use without permission for purposes such as criticism, commentary, education, and parody. Courts evaluate four factors:

1. **Purpose and character of the use**: Commercial vs. educational; transformative vs. reproductive
2. **Nature of the copyrighted work**: Factual works receive less protection than creative ones
3. **Amount used**: Both quantitatively and qualitatively (using the "heart" of a work weighs against fair use)
4. **Market effect**: Whether the use harms the market for or value of the original

Fair use is determined case-by-case; no bright-line rules exist. The transformative use analysis has become increasingly important in modern fair use decisions.
