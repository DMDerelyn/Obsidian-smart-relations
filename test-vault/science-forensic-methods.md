# Forensic Science Methods

Forensic science applies scientific principles to legal matters, providing objective evidence for criminal and civil investigations. The field spans multiple disciplines, each contributing specialized techniques for analyzing physical evidence.

## DNA Analysis

DNA evidence has transformed criminal investigation since its first use in 1986. Modern techniques are remarkably sensitive:

### Short Tandem Repeat (STR) Analysis
The standard method for forensic DNA profiling. STRs are repeating sequences of 2-6 base pairs at specific chromosomal locations (loci). The FBI's Combined DNA Index System (CODIS) uses **20 core STR loci** to generate profiles:

- Full profiles can distinguish individuals with probabilities exceeding 1 in a trillion
- Partial profiles from degraded samples still provide significant evidential value
- Analysis requires as little as **0.5 nanograms** of DNA (approximately 80 cells)

### Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA)
Used when nuclear DNA is too degraded. mtDNA is more abundant per cell (hundreds of copies vs. two) but provides less discriminating power since it is maternally inherited.

### Rapid DNA Technology
Portable instruments that can generate a DNA profile in **under 2 hours**, enabling real-time identification at booking stations and disaster sites.

## Fingerprint Analysis

Fingerprints remain one of the most reliable forms of forensic identification:

- **Latent prints**: Invisible impressions left by skin oils, developed using powders, chemicals, or light sources
- **Patent prints**: Visible prints left in blood, ink, or other substances
- **Plastic prints**: Three-dimensional impressions in soft materials like clay or wax

The analysis follows the **ACE-V methodology**: Analysis, Comparison, Evaluation, and Verification. Automated Fingerprint Identification Systems (AFIS) can search databases of millions of prints in minutes.

## Digital Forensics

As technology permeates daily life, #digital-evidence has become central to investigations:

### Computer Forensics
- Forensic imaging creates bit-for-bit copies of storage devices
- Deleted file recovery exploits the gap between logical and physical deletion
- Timeline analysis reconstructs user activity from file metadata, logs, and browser history
- Encryption and anti-forensics tools pose ongoing challenges

### Mobile Device Forensics
- Extraction of call logs, messages, GPS data, app data, and photos
- Cloud data acquisition through account credentials or legal process
- Analysis of encrypted messaging applications

### Network Forensics
- Packet capture and analysis of network traffic
- Log analysis from servers, routers, and firewalls
- Tracing cyberattacks through multiple jurisdictions

## Chain of Custody

The **chain of custody** documents every person who handles evidence from collection to courtroom presentation. A broken chain can render evidence inadmissible:

1. **Collection**: Evidence is collected using proper protocols, documented with photographs and notes
2. **Packaging**: Evidence is sealed in tamper-evident containers with unique identifiers
3. **Transfer**: Every handoff is logged with date, time, and signatures
4. **Storage**: Evidence is kept in secure, environmentally controlled facilities
5. **Analysis**: Laboratory examination follows validated protocols with documented quality controls
6. **Presentation**: The complete custody record accompanies evidence to court

Maintaining an unbroken chain of custody is as important as the scientific analysis itself. Without it, even the most compelling evidence may be excluded.
