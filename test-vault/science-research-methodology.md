# Scientific Research Methodology

The scientific method provides a systematic framework for investigating phenomena, acquiring new knowledge, and correcting previous understanding. Rigorous methodology is what separates scientific inquiry from anecdote and speculation.

## The Scientific Method

While often presented as a linear sequence, the scientific method in practice is **iterative and non-linear**:

1. **Observation**: Notice a phenomenon or pattern that demands explanation
2. **Question**: Formulate a specific, testable question about the observation
3. **Literature Review**: Survey existing research to understand what is already known
4. **Hypothesis**: Propose a falsifiable explanation that makes testable predictions
5. **Experiment**: Design and conduct a test of the hypothesis
6. **Analysis**: Evaluate the data using appropriate statistical methods
7. **Conclusion**: Determine whether the data supports or refutes the hypothesis
8. **Communication**: Share findings through publication and peer review

The critical feature of scientific hypotheses is **falsifiability**: a hypothesis must make predictions that could, in principle, be proven wrong. Unfalsifiable claims (e.g., "invisible forces that leave no trace") fall outside the scope of science.

## Experimental Design

Good experimental design controls for confounding variables and produces reliable, reproducible results:

### Key Principles

- **Control groups**: A baseline condition that receives no treatment, against which experimental groups are compared
- **Randomization**: Random assignment of subjects to groups prevents systematic bias
- **Blinding**: Single-blind (subjects unaware of group assignment) or double-blind (both subjects and researchers unaware) designs reduce bias
- **Replication**: Sufficient sample sizes and repeated measurements increase statistical power
- **Operationalization**: Abstract concepts must be defined in measurable terms

### Common Designs

- **Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT)**: Gold standard for causal inference
- **Cohort study**: Following groups over time to observe outcomes
- **Case-control study**: Comparing individuals with and without a condition retrospectively
- **Cross-sectional study**: Observing a population at a single point in time
- **Meta-analysis**: Systematic statistical combination of results from multiple studies

## Peer Review

Peer review is the process by which scientific work is evaluated by **independent experts** before publication:

- Submitted manuscripts are reviewed by 2-4 experts in the field
- Reviewers assess methodology, analysis, interpretation, and significance
- Reviews may recommend acceptance, revision, or rejection
- The process aims to catch errors, improve clarity, and ensure standards

Criticisms of peer review include slowness, bias toward established paradigms, and the difficulty of detecting fraud. Despite these limitations, it remains the primary quality control mechanism in science.

## Statistical Significance

Statistical analysis determines whether observed results are likely due to the hypothesized effect or merely #random-chance:

### P-values
- The probability of observing results at least as extreme as those obtained, assuming the null hypothesis is true
- Convention: p < 0.05 is considered "statistically significant"
- A low p-value does not prove the hypothesis is true; it only suggests the null hypothesis is unlikely
- P-values are widely misinterpreted; they do not measure the probability that the hypothesis is correct

### Confidence Intervals
- A range of values within which the true population parameter likely falls
- A 95% confidence interval means: if the experiment were repeated many times, 95% of calculated intervals would contain the true value
- More informative than p-values alone because they convey effect size and precision

### Effect Size
- Measures the **magnitude** of a difference or relationship, independent of sample size
- Common measures: Cohen's d, Pearson's r, odds ratios
- A statistically significant result may have a trivially small effect size, making it practically meaningless

The replication crisis in psychology and other fields has highlighted the importance of adequate sample sizes, pre-registration of hypotheses, and transparent reporting of all results, not just statistically significant ones.
