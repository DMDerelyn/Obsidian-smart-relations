# Character Development

Strong characters are the engine of memorable fiction. Readers connect with characters who feel real, who want things desperately, who struggle and change. This note covers the essential elements of character development.

## Character Arcs

A character arc is the transformation a character undergoes through the story. There are three primary types:

### Positive Arc (Change Arc)
The character begins with a **false belief** (sometimes called a "lie") and gradually discovers the **truth** through the events of the story. By the climax, they have shed the lie and embraced the truth, becoming a better version of themselves.

*Example*: A cynical loner learns to trust others and finds strength in community.

### Negative Arc (Fall Arc)
The character begins with truth but is gradually corrupted or broken by the story's events, ending in a worse state than they began.

*Example*: An idealistic leader compromises their values one step at a time until they become the villain.

### Flat Arc (Testing Arc)
The character already holds the truth and does not change. Instead, they **change the world around them** by steadfastly holding to their beliefs despite pressure.

*Example*: A righteous detective refuses to be corrupted despite systemic pressure, eventually reforming the department.

## Motivation

Every character needs a clear motivation --- something they **want** (external goal) and something they **need** (internal growth). The tension between want and need drives compelling storytelling.

Questions to define motivation:
- What does the character want *more than anything*?
- What are they willing to sacrifice to get it?
- What do they actually need to become whole?
- How does their want conflict with their need?

## Flaws and Vulnerabilities

Perfect characters are boring. Flaws create #character-conflict and make characters relatable:

- **Moral flaws**: Selfishness, cowardice, cruelty, dishonesty
- **Psychological flaws**: Fear, insecurity, trauma, obsession
- **Social flaws**: Arrogance, distrust, inability to communicate
- **Blind spots**: Things the character cannot see about themselves

The best flaws are directly connected to the character's arc. A character afraid of vulnerability must eventually be vulnerable to achieve their goal.

## Showing Growth

Character growth must be **demonstrated through action**, not merely stated. Techniques include:

1. **Mirror scenes**: Place the character in a similar situation early and late in the story; their different response shows growth
2. **Escalating choices**: Force progressively harder decisions that test the character's evolving values
3. **Dialogue shifts**: The character's speech patterns, vocabulary, and tone change subtly over time
4. **Relationship evolution**: How the character treats others reflects their internal state

## Supporting Characters

Secondary characters should serve the protagonist's arc while having their own desires and agency. They can function as:

- **Foils**: Characters who contrast with the protagonist, highlighting their traits
- **Mirrors**: Characters on a similar journey, showing an alternative path
- **Catalysts**: Characters who provoke change in the protagonist
- **Anchors**: Characters who represent what the protagonist stands to lose or gain
