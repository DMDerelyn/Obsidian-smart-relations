# Poetry Techniques

Poetry distills language to its most concentrated form. Unlike prose, where meaning accumulates over paragraphs and chapters, a poem must achieve its effect in a compressed space where every word carries maximum weight.

## Meter and Rhythm

Meter is the pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables that creates a poem's rhythmic backbone:

- **Iambic** (da-DUM): The most natural English meter. "Shall I com-PARE thee TO a SUM-mer's DAY"
- **Trochaic** (DUM-da): Creates a falling, urgent rhythm. "TY-ger TY-ger BURN-ing BRIGHT"
- **Anapestic** (da-da-DUM): Galloping, energetic. "'Twas the NIGHT before CHRIST-mas"
- **Dactylic** (DUM-da-da): Rolling, majestic. "THIS is the FOR-est pri-ME-val"
- **Spondaic** (DUM-DUM): Heavy emphasis, used sparingly. "HEART-BREAK"

Lines are named by their number of feet: **pentameter** (5), **tetrameter** (4), **hexameter** (6). Iambic pentameter is the workhorse of English poetry, from Shakespeare's sonnets to Frost's blank verse.

## Rhyme

Beyond simple end rhyme, poets employ sophisticated sound patterns:

- **Perfect rhyme**: Identical vowel and final consonant sounds (moon/June)
- **Slant rhyme**: Near-matches that create subtle resonance (moon/mine, home/come)
- **Internal rhyme**: Rhymes within a line rather than at the end
- **Assonance**: Repeated vowel sounds ("the rain in Spain")
- **Consonance**: Repeated consonant sounds ("pitter patter")
- **Alliteration**: Repeated initial consonant sounds ("wild and woolly")

## Imagery and Sensory Language

Strong poetry activates the senses. The five types of imagery map to the senses:

1. **Visual**: What we see --- color, shape, light, movement
2. **Auditory**: What we hear --- sound, silence, music, noise
3. **Tactile**: What we feel --- texture, temperature, pressure
4. **Olfactory**: What we smell --- fragrance, stench, subtlety
5. **Gustatory**: What we taste --- sweetness, bitterness, flavor

The best imagery is **specific and concrete** rather than abstract. "A red wheelbarrow glazed with rain" works better than "a colorful farming tool."

## Metaphor and Figurative Language

Figurative language creates meaning through comparison and association:

- **Metaphor**: Direct identification ("Life is a broken-winged bird" --- Hughes)
- **Simile**: Comparison using like/as ("My love is like a red, red rose" --- Burns)
- **Personification**: Giving human qualities to non-human things
- **Synecdoche**: Part standing for whole ("All hands on deck")
- **Metonymy**: Associated thing standing for another ("The crown decreed")

## Free Verse

Free verse abandons regular meter and rhyme but is not formless. It uses:

- **Line breaks** for pacing and emphasis
- **White space** for silence and breath
- **Repetition** and #poetic-rhythm for structure
- **Enjambment** (carrying a sentence across line breaks) for momentum

As T.S. Eliot noted, "No verse is free for the man who wants to do a good job." Even in free verse, every choice of lineation and diction must be deliberate.
