# Story Structure

Understanding story structure gives writers a framework for pacing, tension, and emotional impact. While not every story follows a rigid formula, these patterns appear across cultures and centuries of storytelling.

## Three-Act Structure

The most common Western narrative framework divides a story into three parts:

### Act I: Setup (roughly 25% of the story)
- Introduce the **protagonist** and their ordinary world
- Establish the **stakes** and what the character wants
- Present the **inciting incident** that disrupts the status quo
- End with the **first plot point**: the protagonist commits to the central conflict

### Act II: Confrontation (roughly 50% of the story)
- The protagonist faces **escalating obstacles**
- A **midpoint reversal** shifts the story's direction or raises the stakes
- Subplots develop and interweave with the main plot
- The protagonist undergoes growth or change under pressure
- Ends with the **second plot point**: the darkest moment or a critical revelation

### Act III: Resolution (roughly 25% of the story)
- The **climax**: the protagonist confronts the central conflict directly
- Subplots converge and resolve
- The **denouement**: aftermath, new equilibrium, and thematic closure

## The Hero's Journey (Monomyth)

Joseph Campbell identified a universal pattern in mythology, later adapted by Christopher Vogler for screenwriting:

1. **Ordinary World** - The hero's normal life before the adventure
2. **Call to Adventure** - A challenge or quest presents itself
3. **Refusal of the Call** - The hero hesitates or resists
4. **Meeting the Mentor** - A guide provides wisdom or tools
5. **Crossing the Threshold** - The hero enters the unfamiliar world
6. **Tests, Allies, Enemies** - The hero faces challenges and builds relationships
7. **Approach to the Inmost Cave** - Preparation for the major ordeal
8. **The Ordeal** - The hero faces their greatest challenge
9. **Reward** - The hero gains what they sought
10. **The Road Back** - The hero begins the return journey
11. **Resurrection** - A final test that transforms the hero
12. **Return with the Elixir** - The hero brings something valuable home

## Rising Action and Tension

Effective stories build tension through #rising-action, where each scene raises the stakes or narrows the protagonist's options. Techniques include:

- **Ticking clocks**: Deadlines that create urgency
- **Complications**: New problems layered onto existing ones
- **Reversals**: Moments where progress is undone
- **Revelations**: Information that reframes the conflict

The key is that tension should generally **escalate** rather than remain flat. Even quiet character moments should carry underlying tension that propels the reader forward.
