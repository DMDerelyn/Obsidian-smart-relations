# Worldbuilding Guide for Writers

Worldbuilding is the craft of constructing an imaginary setting that feels lived-in and believable. Whether you are writing a novel, designing a tabletop RPG campaign, or creating a screenplay, the principles of effective worldbuilding remain consistent.

## Geography and Environment

The physical world shapes everything else. Start with the big picture:

- **Climate zones**: Determine weather patterns, available resources, and how people live
- **Natural barriers**: Mountains, rivers, oceans, and deserts define borders and trade routes
- **Resource distribution**: Where are metals, fertile land, fresh water, and timber? These drive economies and conflicts
- **Flora and fauna**: The ecosystem should feel coherent; consider food chains and how creatures interact

A world's geography directly influences its cultures. Desert peoples develop differently from forest dwellers, and coastal communities differ from landlocked ones.

## Culture and Society

Cultures should feel distinct yet internally consistent. For each major culture, consider:

### Social Structure
- Who holds power and why? (Aristocracy, meritocracy, theocracy, merchant class)
- What is the family unit? (Nuclear, extended, clan-based, communal)
- How rigid are social classes? Can individuals move between them?

### Values and Beliefs
- What does this culture consider virtuous? Shameful?
- What are their creation myths and religious practices?
- How do they view outsiders, magic, technology, death?

### Art and Expression
- What forms of art, music, and literature are valued?
- How do people celebrate, mourn, and mark milestones?

## Economy

A believable economy grounds #fantasy-worldbuilding in practical reality:

- **Currency systems**: Coins, barter, credit, magical tokens
- **Trade goods**: What does each region produce and what does it need?
- **Labor**: Who does the work? Guilds, serfs, slaves, magical constructs?
- **Wealth distribution**: Is wealth concentrated or spread? This affects social stability

## Magic Systems

If your world includes magic, define its rules:

### Hard Magic Systems
- Clear, well-defined rules and limitations
- Magic has a **cost** (physical, material, or moral)
- Readers can predict and understand what magic can do
- *Examples*: Sanderson's Allomancy, D&D's spell slot system

### Soft Magic Systems
- Rules are vague or unknown to the reader
- Magic feels mysterious and wondrous
- Should not be used as a convenient solution to problems (deus ex machina)
- *Examples*: Tolkien's magic, Earthsea's true names

The key principle is **Sanderson's First Law**: the ability of magic to solve problems in a satisfying way is directly proportional to how well the reader understands it.

## Integration

The best worldbuilding is **iceberg worldbuilding** --- you develop far more than you show. The depth exists beneath the surface, informing details and making the world feel real even when readers only see fragments. Every element should serve the story, not exist merely to showcase your creativity.
